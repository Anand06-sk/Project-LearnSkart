About ME3391 Engineering Thermodynamics

ME3391 Engineering Thermodynamics is a core subject for Mechanical Engineering students in Semester 3 at Anna University. This subject introduces you to the fundamental laws of thermodynamics, energy interactions, and the behavior of pure substances and gases in engineering systems. These ME3391 notes are designed to help you understand key concepts through clear explanations and practical examples.

With these Anna University study materials, you will learn to analyze energy conversion processes, grasp the importance of entropy, and apply thermodynamic cycles to real-world engineering problems. The content also covers steam properties, phase changes, and the use of steam tables and Mollier charts for calculations. These resources are structured for progressive learning and exam preparation.

Important Topics

ME3391 – ENGINEERING THERMODYNAMICS
IMPORTANT TOPICS (EXAM FOCUS)

UNIT I – BASICS & FIRST LAW

  • Thermodynamic system & properties 🔥 Priority
  • Zeroth law & temperature scales
  • First law (closed system applications) 🔥 Priority
  • Steady flow energy equation (SFEE)

UNIT II – SECOND LAW & ENTROPY

  • Second law statements (Kelvin-Planck & Clausius)
  • Carnot cycle & efficiency 🔥 Priority
  • Entropy concept & entropy change 🔥 Priority
  • T–S diagram basics

UNIT III – AVAILABILITY & IRREVERSIBILITY

  • Entropy principle (increase of entropy)
  • Availability & irreversibility 🔥 Priority
  • First & Second law efficiency

UNIT IV – STEAM & PHASE CHANGE

  • Steam properties & phase change
  • Dryness fraction (numericals important) 🔥 Priority
  • Steam tables & Mollier chart usage
  • Work & heat calculations (flow processes)

UNIT V – REAL GASES & APPLICATIONS

  • Ideal vs real gas
  • Compressibility factor & corresponding states
  • Joule-Thomson effect 🔥 Priority
  • Clausius-Clapeyron equation

🚨 MOST SCORING TOPICS (FOCUS FIRST)

  • First law + SFEE
  • Carnot cycle & entropy
  • Steam tables & dryness fraction
  • Availability & irreversibility
  • Joule-Thomson effect
